Japanese culture in business
Business Meetings
Greetings in business meetings

When meeting someone, it is important to make a good impression on the person, especially while doing business, which is why the way you greet and start a business-related discussion in Japan is important. Two popular greetings in Japan are bowing and handshakes.
 
Business Cards

Exchanging business cards (also known as ‘meishi’ in Japan) is a very important part of a business meeting in Japan, and business cards are often seen as an extension of the person’s identity. You should use both hands to give and receive business cards. Upon receiving a card, you should not immediately put it in your pockets, instead, you should read it for a bit and then keep it in order to be polite and respectful. Usually, a slight bow is also performed in order to be respectful during this exchange.
 
Silence and modesty

Unlike many other cultures, talking a lot in the workplace is not the norm and they believe that 'Silence Is Golden'. Instead of talking in overabundance, you should try to remain calm and silent as it shows wisdom, patience, and self-control, however, you should still speak when it is necessary. Moreover, modesty is also valued in Japanese culture, you should not speak too loudly and not gesticulate too much. This helps in giving a modest, humble, and calm impression.
 
 
Respect for Age and Status

One must remember that in Japan, 'Age Equals Seniority', this means that you should respect the age of a person, and show your senior executives added reverance, as the age can be as important as the person's rank in the business. An example of this is to greet the senior-most person in the room before others.
 
Clothing

Though there is no official dress code that one must follow, there is an 'unofficial dress code' that people normally follow in business meetings in Japan. Men typicall wear conservative business suits and blend in with the group, a classic example of this is dark-coloured business suits with ties and white shirts. Women should also dress conservatively and in subdued colours. Moreover, you should avoid wearing a shirt with a black tie underneath, as this is more common for funerals.
 
Gifting

The exchange of gifts is an important part of Japanese business culture, and every opportunity to give a gift should be seized, however, there are some restrictions as to what you should gift, for example, Flowers such as lilies, lotus blossoms and camellias are used for funeral services and therefore should be avoided. The same goes to any white flowers. Potted plants also carry negative superstitions. And a set of four of anything is deemed unlucky. The number nine is also inauspicious. Also, avoid red if you send Christmas cards, as funeral notices are customarily printed in red.
